## Design Context

### Users

**Primary: B2B decision-makers** — municipal officials (mayors, sports department directors), club managers, property developers evaluating whether to host or partner with a Padel Day installation. They visit on desktop during work hours, often skeptical, needing to justify a decision to a council or board. Their job: assess whether this is credible infrastructure worth signing a contract or granting a site for.

**Secondary: Individual padel players** — casual to regular, smartphone-first, curious how automated booking works and whether it's as effortless as promised.

**Core job-to-be-done**: "Convince me in under a minute that this is real, operational, and serious enough to trust with public land / private investment / my Saturday morning."

### Brand Personality

Three words: **trustworthy · effortless · quietly playful**

Emotional goal — the site must land as *"this is serious infrastructure I can trust."* Institutional credibility comes first; the fun comes through in small, confident moments (a well-placed lime accent, a crisp motion detail, a slogan that doesn't try too hard), never through gimmicks that undermine the trust signal.

Tone: confident, concrete, plain-spoken French. "Play Simple" is not ironic — it's a promise about the operational model (open 7h–minuit, no staff, one smartphone, done).

### Aesthetic Direction

**Theme: Light.** Dark mode would read as premium/nocturnal/trader-bro and actively hurt B2B trust with a 60-year-old adjoint au maire. Light mode reads as accessible, institutional, daylight-honest. Pair it with a crisp dark foreground (`--color-dark` #1a1a2e) rather than pure black.

**Reference — yesyespadel.com.** What to channel from it:
- Editorial, magazine-like layout with generous whitespace — not a SaaS template
- Big, confident display type doing most of the work
- Restrained use of accent color; color earns its place
- Photography-forward — real courts, real people, real scale, not stock illustration
- Calm, unhurried pacing — sections breathe

What to do *differently* from yesyespadel (don't mimic, channel):
- Our lime `#d6fd26` is more electric than theirs — use it as the single sharp accent; never wash a whole section in it
- Push the 100% automated angle harder — clean technical iconography, operational confidence (hours, access flow, numbers) treated as reassuring facts, not marketing bullets

**Anti-references — what this must NOT look like:**
- Generic municipal SaaS (stock photo of diverse smiling people + card grid + rounded-rectangle hero)
- Tech-bro startup (dark mode + purple/blue gradient + glowing accents + glassmorphism)
- Sports-facility booking site (cluttered, stock-gym imagery, red/green urgency colors)
- AI slop tells: gradient text, border-left accent stripes, sparklines as decoration, icon-above-every-heading, symmetric card grids everywhere

**Color usage (60/30/10):**
- 60% — warm-tinted off-white surfaces (tint neutrals subtly toward blue to cohere with brand)
- 30% — dark `#1a1a2e` for text + blue `#3164d5` for secondary accents, links, supporting moments
- 10% — lime `#d6fd26` used with discipline: one hero highlight word, primary CTA, and sparing accent moments

**Typography posture:**
- Fredoka (display) carries the "quietly playful" beat — use it large, with real hierarchy contrast
- Poppins (body) carries the institutional trust — tight leading, generous max-width cap at ~68ch
- Do not let Fredoka leak into small UI labels; keep it reserved for headings and one or two hero moments so it stays premium

### Design Principles

1. **Trust first, personality second.** Every element earns its place by reinforcing operational credibility. Playfulness is a spice, never the main course.
2. **Institutional calm, not SaaS frenzy.** Generous whitespace, editorial pacing, one clear message per section. No card-grid sprawl.
3. **Lime is a scalpel, not a paintbrush.** Rare, precise use of `#d6fd26`. When it appears, it matters.
4. **Facts over promises.** "Ouvert 7h–minuit, 7j/7" beats "Available when you want it." Concrete operational details ARE the marketing.
5. **Photography carries the warmth.** Real courts, real French municipal settings. No illustration, no stock gym shots, no AI-generated people.
